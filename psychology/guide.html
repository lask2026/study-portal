Module 1 – History, Approaches, And Methods (11–12%)

College Board Scope: History of psychology, approaches (biological, biopsychosocial, behavioral, cognitive, humanistic, psychodynamic), research methods (experimental, clinical, correlational), ethics in research.

1. What Is Psychology?

Psychology is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes.

  • Behavior = anything observable (talking, crying, running, heart rate)
  • Mental processes = internal experiences (thinking, remembering, feeling, dreaming)

Key point for CLEP:
"Scientific" means systematic observation, measurement, and hypothesis testing, not just common sense or personal opinion.

2. Very Short History You Actually Need

You do not need a huge history timeline, just the big turning points and names.

2.1 From Philosophy to Science

  • Philosophers (e.g., Plato, Aristotle, Descartes) debated the mind, knowledge, and free will. No controlled experiments yet.
  • Wilhelm Wundt (1879) – founded the first psychology lab in Leipzig, Germany. Often called the "father of modern psychology."
  • Used introspection: carefully reporting one's own conscious experiences to simple stimuli
  • His approach is known as structuralism: trying to break consciousness into basic elements (sensations, feelings, images)
  • William James – American psychologist, associated with functionalism
  • Focused on the functions of mental processes: what they do for adaptation and survival, not just their structure

3. The Major Approaches (Perspectives)

You will constantly be asked "Which approach?" on this exam.

3.1 Biological Approach

  • Explains behavior in terms of brain, nervous system, neurotransmitters, hormones, and genes
  • Example: Explaining depression by looking at neurotransmitter levels (e.g., serotonin) or genetic risk

Exam signal words: "brain scans, neurotransmitter imbalance, genetic predisposition, hormones, nervous system"

3.2 Behavioral Approach

  • Focuses only on observable behavior, not internal thoughts
  • Behavior is shaped by learning (conditioning, reinforcement, punishment)
  • Big names: John Watson, B.F. Skinner
  • Example: Child's tantrums increase because they get attention (reinforcement)

Exam signal words: "reinforced, punished, learned, conditioned, stimulus, response"

3.3 Cognitive Approach

  • Focuses on mental processes: thinking, remembering, problem solving, decision making
  • Looks at how people interpret and process information
  • Example: Depression explained by negative thinking patterns ("I'm worthless; nothing will ever change")

Exam signal words: "thoughts, beliefs, expectations, interpretations, problem solving, memory, perception"

3.4 Psychodynamic (Psychoanalytic) Approach

  • Originated with Sigmund Freud
  • Emphasizes:
  • Unconscious conflicts
  • Early childhood experiences
  • Inner struggles between id–ego–superego
  • Defense mechanisms (repression, projection, etc.)
  • Example: A person's fear of dogs is traced back (unconsciously) to a childhood dog attack they "forgot"

Exam signal words: "unconscious, childhood, repressed, conflicts, dreams, Freudian slips"

3.5 Humanistic Approach

  • Emphasizes free will, personal growth, and self-actualization (reaching one's potential)
  • Big names: Carl Rogers, Abraham Maslow
  • Stresses a positive view of humans
  • Example: Therapist focuses on helping client develop a strong, positive self-concept and feel accepted

Exam signal words: "self-actualization, potential, self-concept, unconditional positive regard, personal growth"

3.6 Biopsychosocial Approach

  • Integrates biological, psychological, and social-cultural levels of analysis
  • Example: Depression may involve:
  • Biological: genetic vulnerability, neurotransmitters
  • Psychological: negative thinking
  • Social: stressful life events, lack of support

Exam signal words: "interaction of biological, psychological, and social factors," "multiple levels of analysis"

High-Yield Perspective Summary

If a question's explanation focuses mainly on:

  • Brain/genes/hormones → Biological
  • Learning, reinforcement, punishment → Behavioral
  • Thoughts and interpretations → Cognitive
  • Unconscious conflicts / childhood → Psychodynamic
  • Free will / potential / self-actualization → Humanistic
  • Mix of bio + psycho + social → Biopsychosocial

4. Research Methods – The Heart of the Exam

4.1 Basic Concepts

  • Population: the entire group you care about (e.g., all high school students)
  • Sample: the smaller group you actually study
  • Random sample: every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected

Why it matters: Better sampling → results generalize better to the population

4.2 Experiments: Only Way to Show Cause–Effect

An experiment manipulates at least one variable and holds others constant to find cause and effect.

Key words:

  • Independent variable (IV) – what the researcher manipulates
  • Dependent variable (DV) – what the researcher measures (the "outcome")
  • Experimental group – receives the treatment
  • Control group – does not receive the treatment (or gets placebo)

Random assignment: every participant has equal chance to be in any group. This balances out preexisting differences and is crucial for causation.

If they say "randomly assigned to groups," you are usually looking at an experiment.

4.3 Example – Identify IV, DV, Groups

Study:
"Researchers want to test whether a new memory training app improves recall. They recruit 100 college students. Half are randomly assigned to use the app daily for 2 weeks. The other half uses their phones as usual. At the end, everyone takes a memory test scored from 0–50."

  • IV: Use of memory app vs normal phone use (type of training)
  • DV: Memory test score
  • Experimental group: App group
  • Control group: Normal-use group
  • Random assignment: "Half are randomly assigned …" → indicates experiment

4.4 Correlational Research

A correlation measures how two variables move together, but does not prove cause–effect.

  • Correlation coefficient r ranges from −1 to +1
  • +1: perfect positive relationship
  • −1: perfect negative relationship
  • 0: no linear relationship

Example: Hours spent watching TV and exam scores may correlate. But:

  • Watching TV might lower study time (causal)
  • Poor students might watch more TV to cope (reverse causation)
  • A third variable (e.g., motivation) could affect both

On CLEP, if you see words like "related to," "associated with," or "linked to" with no manipulation, assume correlation and no causation.

4.5 Descriptive Methods

These describe behavior without manipulating variables.

  • Case study – intensive study of a single individual or small group
  • Pros: rich detail; good for rare cases
  • Cons: may not generalize
  • Naturalistic observation – observing behavior in real settings without interference
  • Survey – questionnaires/interviews of many people about attitudes or behaviors

These can show patterns but cannot show cause–effect.

4.6 Ethics in Research

Core principles (especially for human research):

  • Informed consent – participants are told enough about the nature of the study to give voluntary participation
  • Confidentiality – keeping data private
  • Protection from harm – avoiding unnecessary physical or psychological harm
  • Limited, justified deception – allowed only when:
  • necessary,
  • risks are very low,
  • and participants are debriefed afterward
  • Debriefing – full explanation after participation, especially if deception was used

Research Methods and Ethics

CLEP Traps in Methods/Perspectives

  • Confusing random sampling (who you choose from population) with random assignment (which group they go into)
  • Calling any study that "compares groups" an experiment even if there is no manipulation
  • Over-claiming causation from a correlational study
  • Mislabeling an approach:
  • Brain scans → must be biological, not cognitive
  • "Self-actualization" → humanistic, not cognitive or psychodynamic

Module 2 – Biological Bases Of Behavior (8–9%)

College Board Scope: Endocrine system, etiology, functional organization of the nervous system, genetics, neuroanatomy, physiological techniques.

1. Neurons and How They Communicate

1.1 Basic Neuron Structure

A neuron is a nerve cell that sends and receives information.

  • Dendrites – branchlike fibers that receive messages from other neurons
  • Cell body (soma) – contains nucleus; integrates incoming signals
  • Axon – long fiber that carries neural impulses away from cell body
  • Myelin sheath – fatty insulation around axon; speeds conduction
  • Terminal buttons – end of axon; release neurotransmitters into synapse
  • Synapse – gap between neurons where chemical communication occurs

Key process:

  1. Electrical impulse (action potential) travels down axon
  2. Reaches terminal buttons
  3. Neurotransmitters released into synapse
  4. Bind to receptor sites on next neuron's dendrites
  5. Signal continues if threshold is reached

CLEP angle: They like "which part does X?" questions and "what happens at the synapse?" scenarios.

1.2 Major Neurotransmitters (Very Testable)

  • Acetylcholine (ACh) – muscle movement, memory. Low ACh linked with Alzheimer's symptoms
  • Dopamine – movement, reward, attention. Low dopamine → Parkinson's disease; high dopamine activity → schizophrenia-like symptoms
  • Serotonin – mood, sleep, appetite. Low serotonin → depression; many antidepressants increase serotonin
  • Norepinephrine – alertness, arousal, mood
  • GABA – main inhibitory transmitter; low levels linked with seizures, anxiety
  • Glutamate – main excitatory transmitter; excess may lead to migraines or seizures

High-yield: Match each transmitter to one classic disorder and one function.

2. The Nervous System: Organization

2.1 Two Big Divisions

  • Central nervous system (CNS) – brain and spinal cord
  • Peripheral nervous system (PNS) – all nerves outside CNS

PNS splits into:

  • Somatic nervous system – controls voluntary skeletal muscles; carries sensory info and motor commands
  • Autonomic nervous system – controls glands and internal organs (heart, digestion)

Autonomic splits into:

  • Sympathetic – "fight or flight"
  • Increases heart rate, blood pressure, breathing
  • Dilates pupils; slows digestion
  • Mobilizes body for action
  • Parasympathetic – "rest and digest"
  • Decreases heart rate, breathing
  • Stimulates digestion; conserves energy
  • Calms body down

CLEP trap: Sympathetic = mobilize/arouse, Parasympathetic = calm/restore. Don't reverse them.

3. Neuroanatomy – Brain Structures

3.1 Lower Structures

  • Brainstem – basic survival functions
  • Medulla – breathing, heartbeat
  • Pons – sleep, arousal, bridge between regions
  • Cerebellum – balance, coordination, fine motor control; also involved in procedural learning

3.2 Limbic System (Emotion and Memory)

  • Amygdala – emotion, especially fear and aggression
  • Hippocampus – forming new explicit memories (facts/events)
  • Hypothalamus – homeostasis (body temperature, hunger, thirst), controls endocrine system via the pituitary, involved in sex drive and reward

3.3 Cerebral Cortex and Lobes

  • Frontal lobes
  • Planning, decision making, judgment, personality
  • Motor cortex – controls voluntary movement
  • Damage may cause personality change, poor judgment
  • Parietal lobes
  • Somatosensory cortex – processes touch, pain, temperature
  • Spatial processing
  • Temporal lobes
  • Hearing, language comprehension
  • Memory processing
  • Occipital lobes
  • Vision; primary visual cortex

4. Endocrine System (Hormones)

Endocrine system – glands that secrete hormones into the bloodstream.

  • Pituitary gland – "master gland," controlled by hypothalamus; regulates other glands, growth hormone
  • Adrenal glands – above kidneys; release adrenaline (epinephrine) and cortisol in stress response
  • Thyroid gland – regulates metabolism
  • Gonads (ovaries/testes) – sex hormones (estrogen, testosterone)

Key idea: Hormones are slower-acting but longer-lasting than neurotransmitters.

5. Genetics and Etiology

  • Genotype – genetic makeup
  • Phenotype – observable characteristics (result of genes + environment)
  • Heritability – proportion of variation in a trait in a population attributable to genetic differences (not "how genetic my trait is")
  • Etiology – causes or origins of a disorder or trait

Psychological traits and disorders usually arise from gene–environment interactions, not genes alone.

Twin and adoption studies: If identical twins are more similar than fraternal twins, genetic influence is suggested. If adopted children resemble biological parents more than adoptive parents, genetic influence is suggested.

6. Physiological Techniques (Brain Imaging)

  • EEG (electroencephalogram) – records electrical activity of brain; measures brain waves; good for sleep research, seizure detection
  • CT (computed tomography) scan – X-ray images; shows brain structure
  • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) – detailed images of brain structure using magnetic fields
  • fMRI (functional MRI) – shows brain activity by measuring blood flow; indicates which areas are active during tasks
  • PET (positron emission tomography) scan – shows brain activity by tracking radioactive glucose; indicates metabolic activity

High-yield distinction:

  • Structure (anatomy): CT, MRI
  • Function (activity): EEG, fMRI, PET

Module 3 – Sensation And Perception (7–8%)

College Board Scope: Attention, other senses (somesthesis, olfaction, gustation, vestibular), perceptual development, perceptual processes, receptor processes (vision and audition), sensory mechanisms (thresholds and adaptation).

1. Sensation vs Perception

  • Sensation – the process of receiving raw sensory input from the environment through sensory receptors
  • Perception – the process of organizing and interpreting that sensory input to give it meaning

Example: Light waves hit your retina (sensation), and you perceive a red apple (perception).

2. Vision Basics

2.1 The Eye and Visual Pathway

Key structures:

  • Cornea – clear protective covering; bends light
  • Pupil – opening that allows light to enter
  • Iris – colored muscle that controls pupil size
  • Lens – focuses light onto retina; accommodation (changing shape)
  • Retina – light-sensitive layer at back of eye containing photoreceptors

Photoreceptors:

  • Rods – detect black, white, gray; function in low light; located in periphery
  • Cones – detect color and fine detail; function in bright light; concentrated in fovea (center of retina)

Transduction: Light energy is converted into neural signals by rods and cones.

Blind spot: Area where optic nerve exits retina; no photoreceptors there.

2.2 Color Vision

Trichromatic theory (Young-Helmholtz): Three types of cones (red, green, blue); color perception from combining signals.

Opponent-process theory: Color perception organized in opposing pairs (red-green, blue-yellow, black-white); explains afterimages.

Both theories are correct at different stages of processing.

3. Audition (Hearing) Basics

Sound waves:

  • Frequency – pitch (high vs low tones)
  • Amplitude – loudness (volume)

Key structures:

  • Outer ear – collects sound waves
  • Eardrum (tympanic membrane) – vibrates in response to sound
  • Ossicles (hammer, anvil, stirrup) – three tiny bones in middle ear; amplify vibrations
  • Cochlea – fluid-filled spiral structure in inner ear; contains hair cells that transduce vibrations into neural signals
  • Auditory nerve – carries signals to brain

Transduction: Vibrations → hair cell movement → neural signals.

4. Thresholds and Adaptation

4.1 Absolute Threshold

Absolute threshold – the minimum stimulation needed to detect a stimulus 50% of the time.

Example: The faintest sound you can hear in a quiet room.

4.2 Difference Threshold (Just Noticeable Difference – JND)

Difference threshold – the minimum change in stimulation needed to detect that a change has occurred 50% of the time.

Weber's Law: The size of the JND is proportional to the intensity of the original stimulus (e.g., detecting a 1-pound difference is easier in a 10-pound weight than in a 100-pound weight).

4.3 Sensory Adaptation

Sensory adaptation – decreased sensitivity to a constant, unchanging stimulus.

Example: You stop noticing the hum of a refrigerator after a few minutes.

CLEP trap: Don't confuse adaptation (stop noticing) with absolute threshold (minimum to detect).

5. Attention

  • Selective attention – focusing on one stimulus while filtering out others
  • Inattentional blindness – failing to see visible objects when attention is directed elsewhere (e.g., gorilla in basketball video)
  • Change blindness – failing to notice changes in a scene

6. Other Senses

  • Somesthesis (somatosensation) – touch, pressure, temperature, pain
  • Olfaction – smell; closely linked to memory and emotion
  • Gustation – taste; five basic tastes (sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami)
  • Vestibular sense – balance and body position; inner ear (semicircular canals)
  • Kinesthesis – sense of body position and movement of muscles and joints

7. Perceptual Organization

7.1 Gestalt Principles

Gestalt psychology: "The whole is greater than the sum of its parts." We organize sensory input into meaningful wholes.

Key principles:

  • Figure-ground – distinguishing an object (figure) from its surroundings (ground)
  • Proximity – objects close together are grouped together
  • Similarity – objects that look similar are grouped together
  • Continuity – we perceive smooth, continuous patterns
  • Closure – we fill in gaps to perceive complete objects

7.2 Depth Perception

Binocular cues (require both eyes):

  • Retinal disparity – slight difference in images from each eye; brain uses this to judge distance
  • Convergence – eyes turn inward for nearby objects

Monocular cues (work with one eye):

  • Relative size – smaller objects appear farther away
  • Interposition (overlap) – objects that block others appear closer
  • Linear perspective – parallel lines converge in distance
  • Texture gradient – detailed textures appear closer; less detail farther
  • Relative height – objects higher in visual field appear farther
  • Motion parallax – nearby objects move faster across vision than distant objects

7.3 Perceptual Constancies

  • Size constancy – perceiving objects as same size despite changes in retinal image size (person far away still seems normal-sized)
  • Shape constancy – perceiving objects as same shape despite changes in viewing angle (door appears rectangular even when open)
  • Color/Brightness constancy – perceiving colors as constant under different lighting

Sensation, Perception & Consciousness

CLEP Traps (Sensation/Perception)

  • Confusing absolute threshold (detecting a stimulus) with difference threshold (detecting a change)
  • Mixing up sensory adaptation ("stop noticing") with sensory damage
  • Choosing "cones" for peripheral night vision (should be rods)
  • Misattributing vestibular sense to cochlea instead of semicircular canals

Module 4 – States Of Consciousness (5–6%)

College Board Scope: Hypnosis and meditation, psychoactive drug effects, sleep and dreaming.

1. Sleep and Sleep Stages

1.1 Sleep Architecture

Sleep cycle: Approximately 90 minutes, cycling through stages multiple times per night.

NREM (Non-Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep:

  • Stage 1 – light sleep; transitional; easy to wake; theta waves
  • Stage 2 – slightly deeper; sleep spindles and K-complexes on EEG; most of sleep time
  • Stage 3 – deep sleep (slow-wave sleep); delta waves; hard to wake; restorative

REM (Rapid Eye Movement) Sleep:

  • Rapid eye movements, vivid dreams
  • Brain activity similar to waking
  • Muscle paralysis (except eyes and diaphragm)
  • More REM later in night; more deep sleep early in night

1.2 Functions of Sleep

  • Restoration – body and brain repair
  • Memory consolidation – especially REM for procedural; deep sleep for declarative
  • Growth – growth hormone released during deep sleep
  • Immune function – strengthens immune system

1.3 Sleep Disorders

  • Insomnia – difficulty falling or staying asleep
  • Narcolepsy – sudden, uncontrollable sleep attacks; may include cataplexy (muscle weakness)
  • Sleep apnea – breathing interruptions during sleep; leads to poor sleep quality and daytime fatigue
  • Night terrors – intense fear during deep sleep (NREM-3); person doesn't remember; most common in children
  • Sleepwalking (somnambulism) – walking or performing activities during deep sleep; no memory of event

2. Dreams

2.1 Dream Theories

Freud's psychoanalytic theory:

  • Manifest content – literal, surface story of the dream
  • Latent content – hidden, symbolic meaning (unconscious wishes)

Activation-synthesis theory (Hobson & McCarley):

  • REM sleep activates random neural activity
  • Brain tries to make sense of this activity by synthesizing it into a story
  • Dreams have no hidden meaning; they are byproducts of brain activity

3. Hypnosis

Hypnosis – a state of focused attention and increased suggestibility.

What hypnosis can do:

  • Reduce pain perception
  • Enhance relaxation
  • Assist with some habit changes (when motivated)

What hypnosis CANNOT do:

  • Force people to act against their will
  • Reliably recover accurate repressed memories (actually increases false memories)
  • Give people superhuman abilities

CLEP trap: Hypnosis does NOT guarantee accurate memory recall; it often increases confidence in false memories.

4. Meditation

Meditation – practice of focusing attention to achieve a mentally clear, emotionally calm state.

Benefits:

  • Reduced stress and anxiety
  • Improved attention and focus
  • Emotional regulation
  • Physical relaxation

5. Psychoactive Drugs

Psychoactive drugs – chemicals that alter perceptions, mood, thinking, or behavior by affecting neurotransmitter systems.

5.1 Depressants

Effect: Slow down (depress) neural activity; reduce arousal.

  • Alcohol – impairs judgment, coordination, memory; enhances GABA
  • Barbiturates – sedatives; dangerous when combined with alcohol
  • Benzodiazepines – anti-anxiety drugs (e.g., Valium, Xanax); enhance GABA

5.2 Stimulants

Effect: Speed up (stimulate) neural activity; increase arousal and energy.

  • Caffeine – blocks adenosine (which promotes sleep); increases alertness
  • Nicotine – increases dopamine and norepinephrine; highly addictive
  • Cocaine – blocks reuptake of dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine; euphoria, then crash
  • Amphetamines – increase dopamine and norepinephrine; used medically for ADHD

5.3 Hallucinogens

Effect: Alter perceptions, thoughts, and feelings; distort sensory experiences.

  • LSD – powerful hallucinogen; alters serotonin; vivid perceptual distortions
  • Marijuana (cannabis) – THC affects cannabinoid receptors; relaxation, altered time perception, increased appetite; classified as mild hallucinogen or mixed-effect drug

5.4 Opioids

Effect: Pain relief (analgesia); produce euphoria by mimicking endorphins.

  • Morphine, heroin, oxycodone, fentanyl
  • Highly addictive; risk of overdose and death

5.5 Key Drug Concepts

  • Tolerance – need increasing amounts to achieve same effect
  • Dependence – physical or psychological need for the drug
  • Physical dependence – body adapts; withdrawal symptoms if stopped
  • Psychological dependence – craving and compulsion to use
  • Withdrawal – unpleasant symptoms when drug use stops

CLEP Traps (Consciousness)

  • Confusing REM with deep, slow-wave sleep (deep sleep is NREM-3)
  • Treating marijuana as a pure depressant (often classified as hallucinogen or mixed)
  • Thinking hypnosis gives perfect memory (actually increases false memories)
  • Mislabeling alcohol as a stimulant because people seem energized (it's a depressant that lowers inhibitions)

Module 5 – Learning (8–9%)

College Board Scope: Biological bases, classical conditioning, cognitive processes in learning, observational learning, operant conditioning.

Learning is a huge part of the test because it's easy to write scenario questions and every intro psych course covers it heavily.

1. Classical Conditioning – Making Associations

Classical conditioning = learning to associate two stimuli so that one predicts the other.

Famous example: Pavlov's dogs

1.1 Key Terms

  • Unconditioned stimulus (UCS) – naturally triggers a response (food)
  • Unconditioned response (UCR) – natural response to UCS (salivation to food)
  • Neutral stimulus (NS) – does not yet trigger the desired response (bell at first)
  • Conditioned stimulus (CS) – previously NS, now triggers response after pairing with UCS (bell after learning)
  • Conditioned response (CR) – learned response to CS (salivation to bell)

1.2 Process

1. Before conditioning:

  • UCS (food) → UCR (salivation)
  • NS (bell) → no salivation

2. During conditioning (acquisition):

  • NS (bell) + UCS (food) presented together repeatedly

3. After conditioning:

  • CS (bell) → CR (salivation)

1.3 Other Important Concepts

  • Acquisition – initial learning of the association
  • Extinction – when the CS is presented alone repeatedly, the CR weakens and disappears
  • Spontaneous recovery – CR briefly reappears after a pause following extinction
  • Generalization – CR occurs to stimuli similar to CS (different bell tone also causes salivation)
  • Discrimination – CR occurs only to specific CS, not to similar stimuli (only this bell, not others)

2. Operant Conditioning – Consequences Shape Behavior

Operant conditioning = behavior is strengthened or weakened by consequences (rewards/punishments). Associated with B.F. Skinner.

2.1 Types of Consequences

Two key questions:

  1. Does the consequence increase or decrease the behavior?
  2. Does the consequence involve adding something or removing something?

  • Reinforcement → makes behavior more likely
  • Punishment → makes behavior less likely
  • Positive → add something
  • Negative → remove something

2.2 Examples

Positive reinforcement:

  • You study → you get praise or money
  • Behavior (studying) increases

Negative reinforcement:

  • You buckle your seatbelt → annoying buzzer stops
  • Removing something unpleasant (buzzer) increases seatbelt use

Positive punishment:

  • You tease your sibling → you get yelled at
  • Yelling is added; teasing decreases

Negative punishment:

  • You break curfew → your parents take away your phone
  • Something desirable removed; curfew-breaking decreases

CLEP trap: Many students think "negative reinforcement" means punishment. It is reinforcement (behavior increases).

2.3 Schedules of Reinforcement

Continuous reinforcement – reinforce every correct response

Partial (intermittent) reinforcement – reinforce only sometimes

Partial schedules (most tested):

  • Fixed ratio (FR) – reinforcement after a set number of responses
  • Example: Paid after making 10 items
  • Variable ratio (VR) – reinforcement after a changing number of responses, around an average
  • Example: Slot machines
  • Fixed interval (FI) – reinforcement after a set time interval
  • Example: Weekly paycheck
  • Variable interval (VI) – reinforcement after a changing time interval, around an average
  • Example: Checking for random emails; pop quizzes

High-yield pattern:

  • VR produces very high, steady responding and is most resistant to extinction
  • Continuous reinforcement → fast learning but easy extinction

3. Observational Learning (Social Learning)

Associated with Albert Bandura.

  • People can learn by watching others (models)
  • Bobo doll experiment: children watched an adult behave aggressively toward a doll
  • Those who saw aggressive behavior were more likely to imitate it, especially if the model was rewarded

Key terms:

  • Modeling – imitating the behavior of a model
  • Vicarious reinforcement/punishment – learning from seeing others being reinforced or punished

4. Cognitive and Biological Influences on Learning

4.1 Cognitive Elements

  • Latent learning – learning that occurs but is not shown until there is motivation to demonstrate it
  • Example: A rat learns a maze without reward; later, when given food reward, it suddenly performs very well (it had formed a cognitive map)
  • Insight learning – sudden understanding of a solution ("aha!" moment)

4.2 Biological Constraints

  • Taste aversion – easily learning to avoid a food associated with illness, even after one pairing
  • Animals are biologically prepared to learn some associations more easily than others
  • Instinctive drift – learned behavior gradually replaced by instinctive behavior

Learning

CLEP Traps (Learning)

  • Calling negative reinforcement "punishment"
  • If the behavior increases, it is reinforcement, no matter how unpleasant
  • Confusing CS and UCS:
  • CS starts as a neutral stimulus that becomes predictive
  • Ignoring whether the stimulus is added or removed when labeling reinforcement/punishment
  • Forgetting that VR schedules (like gambling) → highest response rate and strong resistance to extinction

Module 6 – Cognition (8–9%)

College Board Scope: Intelligence and creativity, language, memory, thinking and problem solving.

1. Memory Systems

1.1 Three-Stage Model (Atkinson-Shiffrin)

Sensory memory → Short-term/Working memory → Long-term memory

1.2 Sensory Memory

  • Duration: Less than 1 second (visual) to a few seconds (auditory)
  • Capacity: Large; holds all sensory input briefly
  • Function: Initial recording of sensory information
  • Types:
  • Iconic memory – visual sensory memory
  • Echoic memory – auditory sensory memory

1.3 Short-Term Memory (STM) / Working Memory

  • Duration: About 20-30 seconds without rehearsal
  • Capacity: Limited; about 7 ± 2 items (Miller's "magic number")
  • Function: Holds information being actively processed
  • Working memory – modern term emphasizing active manipulation of information (not just storage)

Strategies to increase capacity:

  • Chunking – grouping information into meaningful units (remembering phone number as 555-1234 rather than 5-5-5-1-2-3-4)

1.4 Long-Term Memory (LTM)

  • Duration: Potentially unlimited
  • Capacity: Essentially unlimited
  • Function: Permanent storage

Types of long-term memory:

Explicit (declarative) memory – conscious recall of facts and experiences:

  • Semantic memory – facts and general knowledge (Paris is in France)
  • Episodic memory – personal experiences and events (your 10th birthday)

Implicit (nondeclarative) memory – unconscious memory:

  • Procedural memory – skills and how to do things (riding a bike, typing)
  • Priming – exposure to one stimulus influences response to another
  • Classical conditioning effects

2. Encoding, Storage, and Retrieval

2.1 Encoding (Getting Information In)

Encoding strategies:

  • Rehearsal – repetition
  • Maintenance rehearsal – simple repetition
  • Elaborative rehearsal – relating new info to what you already know (more effective)
  • Chunking – grouping items
  • Mnemonic devices – memory aids (acronyms, imagery, method of loci)
  • Spacing effect – distributed practice better than massed practice (cramming)
  • Levels of processing – deeper, meaningful processing leads to better memory

Encoding specificity: Memory improved when retrieval context matches encoding context.

2.2 Storage

How memories are stored:

  • Neural connections strengthened through long-term potentiation (LTP)
  • Memories distributed across brain regions
  • Hippocampus crucial for consolidating new explicit memories into long-term storage

2.3 Retrieval (Getting Information Out)

Types of retrieval:

  • Recall – retrieving information without cues (essay question)
  • Recognition – identifying previously learned information (multiple choice)
  • Relearning – learning information again; usually faster the second time

Retrieval cues:

  • Context-dependent memory – better recall in same physical environment as encoding
  • State-dependent memory – better recall in same emotional/physiological state as encoding

3. Forgetting

3.1 Why We Forget

Encoding failure – information never entered long-term memory

Storage decay – fading of memory traces over time (less supported now)

Retrieval failure – information is stored but can't be accessed:

  • Tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon – feeling of knowing something but can't retrieve it
  • Interference – other information blocks retrieval

3.2 Interference (Very Testable)

Proactive interference – old information interferes with learning new information

  • Example: Old phone number interferes with remembering new phone number

Retroactive interference – new information interferes with recalling old information

  • Example: Learning French interferes with remembering Spanish learned earlier

Memory aid: PROactive = PROblem with the past interfering forward; RETROactive = recent info goes backward to disrupt old memories

3.3 Memory Construction and Distortion

  • Misinformation effect – incorporating misleading information into memory after an event
  • Source amnesia – remembering information but forgetting where it came from
  • False memories – memories of events that never happened, but feel real
  • Memories are reconstructed, not replayed like a video

4. Thinking and Problem Solving

4.1 Concepts and Prototypes

  • Concept – mental grouping of similar objects, events, ideas
  • Prototype – best example of a concept (robin is prototypical bird; penguin less so)

4.2 Problem-Solving Strategies

Algorithm – step-by-step procedure that guarantees a solution (but may be time-consuming)

Heuristic – mental shortcut; quicker but error-prone

Common heuristics:

  • Availability heuristic – judging likelihood based on how easily examples come to mind
  • Example: Overestimating airplane deaths because plane crashes are memorable
  • Representativeness heuristic – judging likelihood based on how well something matches a prototype
  • Example: Assuming a quiet, bookish person is a librarian rather than a salesperson (ignoring base rates)

4.3 Obstacles to Problem Solving

  • Confirmation bias – seeking information that confirms pre-existing beliefs; ignoring contradictory evidence
  • Fixation – inability to see a problem from a new perspective
  • Functional fixedness – tendency to think of objects only in terms of their usual functions
  • Example: Not thinking to use a coin as a screwdriver
  • Mental set – tendency to approach problems in a way that worked in the past

5. Language

5.1 Components of Language

  • Phonemes – smallest units of sound in a language (e.g., /b/, /a/, /t/)
  • Morphemes – smallest units of meaning (e.g., "un-" "break" "-able")
  • Grammar – rules for combining words
  • Syntax – rules for sentence structure
  • Semantics – meaning of words and sentences

5.2 Language Development

Stages:

  1. Babbling (4-6 months) – producing sounds from all languages
  2. One-word stage (~1 year) – "Mama," "Dog"
  3. Two-word stage (~2 years) – "Want cookie," "Go car" (telegraphic speech)
  4. Rapid expansion – grammar becomes more complex

Theories:

  • Nativist theory (Chomsky) – humans born with language acquisition device (LAD); universal grammar
  • Learning theory – language learned through imitation and reinforcement

Critical period hypothesis – there is a window in childhood when language is most easily acquired.

6. Intelligence and Creativity

6.1 Intelligence

Intelligence – mental ability to learn from experience, solve problems, and adapt to new situations.

IQ (Intelligence Quotient):

  • Mean: 100
  • Standard deviation: 15
  • Normal distribution (bell curve)

Theories of intelligence:

  • Spearman's g factor – general intelligence underlying all mental abilities
  • Gardner's multiple intelligences – 8+ independent intelligences (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic)
  • Sternberg's triarchic theory – analytical, creative, practical intelligence
  • Emotional intelligence – ability to perceive, understand, and manage emotions

6.2 Creativity

Creativity – ability to produce novel and valuable ideas.

  • Divergent thinking – generating many possible solutions (associated with creativity)
  • Convergent thinking – narrowing possibilities to find single best answer

Cognition

CLEP Traps (Cognition)

  • Reversing proactive (old interferes with new) and retroactive (new interferes with old) interference
  • Calling a heuristic an algorithm because it "solves problems" (heuristics are quick shortcuts, not guaranteed)
  • Misclassifying a motor skill (riding a bike) as explicit/semantic instead of implicit/procedural memory
  • Confusing phoneme (smallest sound) with morpheme (smallest unit of meaning)

Module 7 – Motivation And Emotion (5–6%)

College Board Scope: Biological bases, hunger/thirst/sex/pain, social motivation, theories of emotion, theories of motivation.

1. Theories of Motivation

1.1 Drive-Reduction Theory

  • Drive – internal state of tension that motivates behavior to reduce the drive
  • Behavior aimed at maintaining homeostasis (physiological balance)
  • Example: Hunger (drive) → eat (behavior) → reduce hunger

Limitation: Doesn't explain behaviors that increase arousal (e.g., skydiving).

1.2 Arousal Theory

  • People seek an optimal level of arousal
  • Too little arousal → boredom; seek stimulation
  • Too much arousal → anxiety; seek calm

Yerkes-Dodson law: Performance best at moderate arousal; too much or too little impairs performance.

1.3 Incentive Theory

  • Behavior pulled by external incentives (rewards), not just pushed by internal drives
  • Example: Eating dessert even when not hungry (incentive is tasty food)

1.4 Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

Needs organized in pyramid; lower needs must be met before higher needs motivate behavior:

  1. Physiological needs – food, water, shelter
  2. Safety needs – security, stability
  3. Love and belonging – relationships, connection
  4. Esteem needs – achievement, recognition
  5. Self-actualization – realizing full potential, personal growth

CLEP point: Know the order and basic idea.

2. Biological Motivations

2.1 Hunger

Physiological signals:

  • Hypothalamus plays central role
  • Lateral hypothalamus – stimulates hunger ("start eating")
  • Ventromedial hypothalamus – signals satiety ("stop eating")
  • Glucose levels in blood
  • Ghrelin (hormone) – increases hunger
  • Leptin (hormone) – signals satiety

Set point theory: Body has a weight "set point" it tries to maintain.

2.2 Sex

  • Influenced by hormones (testosterone, estrogen)
  • Hypothalamus involved
  • Also strongly influenced by psychological and cultural factors

3. Social and Psychological Motivations

3.1 Achievement Motivation

  • Need for achievement (nAch) – desire to accomplish difficult tasks and meet high standards
  • Varies among individuals

3.2 Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation

Intrinsic motivation – driven by internal satisfaction and interest

  • Example: Reading because you enjoy it

Extrinsic motivation – driven by external rewards or avoiding punishment

  • Example: Reading to get a good grade

Overjustification effect: Giving extrinsic rewards for intrinsically motivated behavior can undermine intrinsic motivation.

4. Theories of Emotion

4.1 James-Lange Theory

Sequence: Stimulus → Physiological arousal → Emotion

  • "I feel afraid because my heart is pounding"
  • Body response comes first, emotion follows

4.2 Cannon-Bard Theory

Sequence: Stimulus → Physiological arousal AND emotion simultaneously

  • Arousal and emotional experience occur at the same time, independently
  • "My heart pounds and I feel afraid at the same time"

4.3 Schachter-Singer Two-Factor Theory

Sequence: Stimulus → Physiological arousal → Cognitive label → Emotion

  • Arousal + interpretation of arousal = emotion
  • Example: Heart pounding + "I'm in danger" = fear; heart pounding + "I'm excited" = excitement
  • Emphasizes cognitive appraisal

4.4 Other Emotion Concepts

  • Facial feedback hypothesis – facial expressions can influence emotional experience (smiling makes you feel happier)
  • Basic emotions – universal emotions (happiness, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, disgust)

Module 8 – Developmental Psychology Across The Lifespan (8–9%)

College Board Scope: Dimensions of development (physical, cognitive, social, moral), gender identity and sex roles, heredity-environment issues, research methods (longitudinal, cross-sectional), theories of development.

1. Research Methods in Developmental Psychology

1.1 Cross-Sectional Design

  • Compare different age groups at one point in time
  • Advantage: Quick, inexpensive
  • Disadvantage: Cannot show changes in same individuals; cohort effects (generational differences may confound age differences)

1.2 Longitudinal Design

  • Follow same individuals over extended period
  • Advantage: Shows actual developmental changes in individuals
  • Disadvantage: Time-consuming, expensive, participant attrition (dropout)

2. Heredity vs Environment (Nature vs Nurture)

  • Nature – genetic inheritance
  • Nurture – environmental influences

Modern view: Interaction of both; not either-or.

Key concepts:

  • Maturation – biological growth processes enabling orderly changes in behavior
  • Critical period – specific time when certain development must occur (if it doesn't, it may never occur)
  • Sensitive period – optimal time for development (but still possible outside this window)

3. Prenatal Development

Stages:

  1. Germinal (0-2 weeks) – fertilization, cell division, implantation
  2. Embryonic (2-8 weeks) – major organs begin to form
  3. Fetal (8 weeks-birth) – growth and maturation

Teratogens – harmful substances that can damage developing embryo/fetus (alcohol, drugs, diseases, radiation).

4. Cognitive Development: Piaget's Theory

Jean Piaget proposed four stages of cognitive development.

4.1 Sensorimotor Stage (0-2 years)

  • Experiencing world through senses and actions
  • Object permanence develops – understanding that objects continue to exist even when not visible (peaks around 8 months)

4.2 Preoperational Stage (2-7 years)

  • Use of symbols (language, pretend play)
  • Egocentrism – difficulty seeing from another's perspective
  • Centration – focus on one aspect, ignoring others
  • Lack conservation – understanding that quantity stays same despite changes in shape

4.3 Concrete Operational Stage (7-11 years)

  • Logical thinking about concrete events
  • Understand conservation (quantity doesn't change with appearance)
  • Can classify and order objects
  • Still struggle with abstract or hypothetical concepts

4.4 Formal Operational Stage (12+ years)

  • Abstract reasoning
  • Hypothetical thinking ("What if...?")
  • Scientific reasoning (test hypotheses systematically)

5. Psychosocial Development: Erikson's Theory

Erik Erikson proposed eight stages, each with a psychosocial crisis.

5.1 The Eight Stages

  1. Trust vs Mistrust (infancy, 0-1) – developing trust in caregivers
  2. Autonomy vs Shame and Doubt (toddler, 1-3) – independence, self-control
  3. Initiative vs Guilt (preschool, 3-6) – taking initiative, planning activities
  4. Industry vs Inferiority (school age, 6-12) – competence, achievement
  5. Identity vs Role Confusion (adolescence, 12-18) – sense of self, identity
  6. Intimacy vs Isolation (young adult, 18-40) – close relationships
  7. Generativity vs Stagnation (middle adult, 40-65) – contributing to society, next generation
  8. Integrity vs Despair (older adult, 65+) – reflecting on life with satisfaction or regret

CLEP: Know the order and basic conflict of each stage.

6. Moral Development: Kohlberg's Theory

Lawrence Kohlberg proposed three levels of moral reasoning.

6.1 Preconventional Level (most children, some adolescents)

  • Stage 1: Obedience and punishment orientation (avoid punishment)
  • Stage 2: Self-interest orientation (what's in it for me?)

6.2 Conventional Level (most adolescents and adults)

  • Stage 3: Good interpersonal relationships ("good boy/girl")
  • Stage 4: Law and order orientation (maintain social order)

6.3 Postconventional Level (some adults)

  • Stage 5: Social contract orientation (rules are social agreements)
  • Stage 6: Universal ethical principles (abstract justice and rights)

High-yield: Match moral reasoning to level (preconventional = punishment/reward; conventional = rules/approval; postconventional = abstract principles).

7. Attachment

Attachment – emotional bond between infant and caregiver.

7.1 Harlow's Monkeys

  • Infant monkeys preferred cloth "mother" (comfort) over wire "mother" with food
  • Shows importance of contact comfort, not just feeding

7.2 Ainsworth's Strange Situation

Attachment styles:

  • Secure attachment – distressed when caregiver leaves; comforted when reunited; uses caregiver as secure base
  • Insecure-avoidant – little distress at separation; avoids caregiver on return
  • Insecure-resistant (ambivalent) – extreme distress at separation; seeks contact but resists comfort
  • Disorganized – confused, contradictory behaviors

High-yield: Secure attachment results from responsive, consistent caregiving.

8. Gender Identity and Sex Roles

  • Sex – biological (male/female based on anatomy, chromosomes)
  • Gender – psychological and social (masculine/feminine based on culture)
  • Gender identity – sense of being male, female, or other
  • Gender roles – expectations about behavior for males and females

Gender-role socialization: Learning culturally appropriate behaviors through observation, reinforcement, and modeling.

Module 9 – Personality (7–8%)

College Board Scope: Assessment techniques, growth and adjustment, personality theories and approaches, self-concept and self-esteem.

1. Major Personality Theories

1.1 Psychodynamic Theory (Freud)

Structure of personality:

  • Id – unconscious; pleasure principle; impulsive desires
  • Ego – conscious; reality principle; mediates between id and superego
  • Superego – internalized moral standards; conscience and ego-ideal

Psychosexual stages:

  • Oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital
  • Fixation at any stage can affect adult personality

Defense mechanisms (unconscious strategies to reduce anxiety):

  • Repression – pushing threatening thoughts into unconscious
  • Projection – attributing one's own unacceptable thoughts to others
  • Displacement – shifting impulses to safer target
  • Regression – reverting to earlier developmental stage
  • Rationalization – creating logical explanations for irrational behavior
  • Sublimation – channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable activities

1.2 Humanistic Theory

Carl Rogers:

  • Emphasis on self-concept – how we see ourselves
  • Unconditional positive regard – accepting and valuing person regardless of behavior
  • Congruence – match between self-concept and actual experience
  • Growth occurs in supportive environment

Abraham Maslow:

  • Self-actualization – realizing one's full potential
  • Hierarchy of needs (see Module 7)

Key themes: Free will, personal growth, positive view of human nature.

1.3 Trait Theory

Personality described by stable, enduring characteristics (traits).

Big Five traits (OCEAN):

  1. Openness – imagination, creativity, curiosity
  2. Conscientiousness – organized, responsible, disciplined
  3. Extraversion – sociable, outgoing, energetic
  4. Agreeableness – cooperative, compassionate, trusting
  5. Neuroticism – anxious, moody, emotionally unstable

High-yield: These traits are relatively stable across situations and time.

1.4 Social-Cognitive Theory (Bandura)

Reciprocal determinism – interaction of:

  • Behavior
  • Personal/cognitive factors
  • Environment

Each influences the others.

Self-efficacy – belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations.

Locus of control (Rotter):

  • Internal locus – believe they control their own fate
  • External locus – believe external forces control fate

Module 10 – Psychological Disorders And Health (8–9%)

College Board Scope: Affective (mood) disorders, anxiety disorders, dissociative disorders, eating disorders, health/stress/coping, personality disorders, psychoses, somatoform disorders, theories of psychopathology.

1. Defining Psychological Disorders

A psychological disorder is a pattern of thoughts, feelings, or behaviors that is:

  1. Deviant – differs from cultural norms
  2. Distressful – causes suffering
  3. Dysfunctional – interferes with daily life

DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, 5th edition) – primary diagnostic system used in the U.S.; uses descriptive criteria, not causes.

2. Major Categories of Disorders

2.1 Anxiety Disorders

Excessive fear and anxiety.

Types:

  • Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) – persistent, excessive worry about many things
  • Panic disorder – recurrent, unexpected panic attacks (intense fear, physical symptoms)
  • Phobias – intense, irrational fear of specific object or situation
  • Specific phobia – fear of specific things (heights, spiders, flying)
  • Social anxiety disorder – fear of social or performance situations
  • Agoraphobia – fear of situations where escape might be difficult

2.2 Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders

  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) – unwanted, repetitive thoughts (obsessions) and/or behaviors (compulsions)
  • Example: Obsession with germs → compulsion to wash hands repeatedly

2.3 Mood (Affective) Disorders

Disturbances in emotional state.

Major depressive disorder:

  • Persistent depressed mood and/or loss of interest
  • Symptoms: hopelessness, fatigue, sleep/appetite changes, difficulty concentrating
  • Duration: At least 2 weeks

Bipolar disorder:

  • Alternating between manic episodes (elevated mood, high energy, impulsivity, decreased need for sleep) and depressive episodes
  • Bipolar I – full manic episodes
  • Bipolar II – hypomania (less severe) + depression

2.4 Psychotic Disorders

Loss of contact with reality.

Schizophrenia spectrum:

Positive symptoms (additions to normal experience):

  • Delusions – false beliefs (persecution, grandeur, reference)
  • Hallucinations – false perceptions (hearing voices most common)
  • Disorganized speech and behavior

Negative symptoms (loss of normal functions):

  • Flat affect (emotional flatness)
  • Social withdrawal
  • Lack of motivation

Dopamine hypothesis: Excess dopamine activity linked to schizophrenia.

2.5 Dissociative Disorders

Disruption in consciousness, memory, or identity.

  • Dissociative amnesia – inability to recall important personal information (not due to brain injury)
  • Dissociative identity disorder (DID) – presence of two or more distinct personality states (formerly "multiple personality disorder")

2.6 Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders

Physical symptoms without clear medical cause (formerly "somatoform disorders").

  • Somatic symptom disorder – excessive concern about physical symptoms
  • Conversion disorder – neurological symptoms (paralysis, blindness) without neurological cause
  • Illness anxiety disorder – preoccupation with having or acquiring serious illness (formerly "hypochondriasis")

2.7 Eating Disorders

Severe disturbances in eating behavior.

  • Anorexia nervosa – restriction of food intake, fear of weight gain, distorted body image; significantly low body weight
  • Bulimia nervosa – binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors (purging, excessive exercise)
  • Binge-eating disorder – recurrent binge eating without compensatory behaviors

2.8 Personality Disorders

Enduring, inflexible patterns of behavior causing distress or impairment.

Cluster A (odd/eccentric):

  • Paranoid, schizoid, schizotypal

Cluster B (dramatic/emotional/erratic):

  • Antisocial personality disorder – disregard for others' rights, lack of remorse
  • Borderline personality disorder – instability in relationships, self-image, emotions; impulsivity
  • Histrionic, narcissistic

Cluster C (anxious/fearful):

  • Avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive personality disorder

3.1 Stress

  1. Alarm – initial shock, arousal
  2. Resistance – body attempts to cope
  3. Exhaustion – resources depleted; vulnerability to illness

3.2 Stress and Health

Chronic stress linked to:

  • Weakened immune system
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Mental health problems

3.3 Coping Strategies

Module 11 – Treatment Of Psychological Disorders (6–7%)

College Board scope: Behavioral therapies; biological and drug therapies; cognitive therapies; community and preventive approaches; insight therapies (psychodynamic and humanistic)

1) Big Picture: 5 Treatment Families

  1. Insight therapies (psychodynamic, humanistic): change by understanding self.
  2. Behavior therapies: change behavior through conditioning principles.
  3. Cognitive therapies: change thinking patterns.
  4. Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT): cognitive + behavioral tools together.
  5. Biological therapies: drugs, brain stimulation, (rare) surgery.

CLEP skill: match the primary target (behavior vs thoughts vs unconscious conflicts vs biology) to the correct approach.

2) Insight Therapies

2.1 Psychodynamic Therapy (Freud-based)

Goal: bring unconscious conflicts into awareness.

Common techniques:

  • Free association (say whatever comes to mind)
  • Dream analysis
  • Interpretation (therapist explains patterns)
  • Transference (client projects feelings about important people onto therapist)

High-Yield bullets:

  • If the stem emphasizes childhood/unconscious/conflicts/defense mechanisms → psychodynamic.

CLEP Trap: Choosing “psychodynamic” for any talk therapy, even when the stem focuses on irrational beliefs (that’s cognitive).

2.2 Humanistic (Client-Centered) Therapy (Rogers)

Goal: growth, self-acceptance, congruence (self-concept matches lived experience).

Therapist stance:

  • Unconditional positive regard (nonjudgmental acceptance)
  • Empathy
  • Genuineness

High-Yield bullets:

  • If the stem says acceptance/empathy/self-actualization → humanistic/client-centered.

CLEP Trap: confusing humanistic therapy with “giving advice.” Client-centered therapy avoids direct advice and focuses on the client’s self-discovery.

3) Behavioral Therapies

Behavior therapies apply learning principles (classical + operant conditioning).

3.1 Classical-conditioning based

  • Systematic desensitization: gradual exposure + relaxation (often for phobias).
  • Exposure therapy: repeated exposure to feared stimulus until fear decreases (extinction).
  • Flooding: intense exposure all at once (faster, more distressing).

High-Yield bullets:

  • Phobia + gradual steps + relaxation → systematic desensitization.
  • Phobia + repeated exposure → exposure therapy.

CLEP Trap: mixing up systematic desensitization (gradual) with flooding (immediate, intense).

3.2 Operant-conditioning based

  • Token economy: earn tokens for desired behaviors; exchange for rewards (often in institutions).
  • Contingency management: rewards tied to behavior (e.g., clean drug tests).
  • Shaping: reinforce successive approximations toward desired behavior.
  • Aversive conditioning: pair unwanted behavior with unpleasant stimulus (historically used for addictions).

High-Yield bullets:

  • Tokens → token economy (operant conditioning).

CLEP Trap: calling a token economy “negative reinforcement” automatically—token economies typically use positive reinforcement.

4) Cognitive Therapies

Goal: identify and change distorted thinking.

  • Beck’s cognitive therapy: target negative thought patterns (depression).
  • REBT (Ellis): dispute irrational beliefs.

High-Yield bullets:

  • If the therapist challenges beliefs/thoughts/interpretations → cognitive therapy.

CLEP Trap: confusing “cognitive therapy” (thought-focused) with “behavior therapy” (action-focused) when both are mentioned. If both are present, it may be CBT.

5) Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT = cognitive restructuring + behavioral change.

  • Uses homework, skills practice, exposure, thought logs.

High-Yield bullets:

  • If the stem includes both thought-challenging AND behavior practice/homework → CBT.

CLEP Trap: picking “behavioral” when the stem includes explicit thinking patterns, or picking “cognitive” when the stem includes structured exposure practice.

6) Biological and Drug Therapies

6.1 Psychopharmacology (Drug therapy)

Common classes (concept-level):

  • Antidepressants (e.g., SSRIs): often target serotonin.
  • Antipsychotics: reduce psychotic symptoms; often affect dopamine.
  • Anxiolytics (anti-anxiety): often enhance GABA.
  • Mood stabilizers (e.g., lithium): bipolar disorder.

High-Yield bullets:

  • Schizophrenia/psychosis → antipsychotics.
  • Depression → antidepressants (SSRIs common).

6.2 Brain stimulation

  • ECT (electroconvulsive therapy): severe, treatment-resistant depression.

CLEP Trap: thinking ECT is a common first-line treatment. On CLEP it’s typically framed as for severe cases.

7) Community & Preventive Approaches

  • Community mental health programs
  • Prevention through education, early intervention, accessible treatment

High-Yield bullets:

  • If the stem mentions community programs/prevention → community/preventive.

Module 12 – Social Psychology (9–10%)

College Board scope: aggression/antisocial behavior; attitudes and attitude change; attribution; conformity/compliance/obedience; group dynamics; attraction; stereotypes/prejudice/discrimination/prosocial behavior.

1) Attribution (How people explain behavior)

  • Internal (dispositional) attribution: behavior due to personality/traits.
  • External (situational) attribution: behavior due to environment.

Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE): overestimate internal causes for others, underestimate situation.

Self-serving bias: successes = internal; failures = external.

CLEP Trap: FAE is usually about other people; self-serving bias is about self.

2) Attitudes and Attitude Change

  • Attitude: evaluation of something (person, idea) positive/negative.

Persuasion routes (ELM):

  • Central route: logic/evidence; lasting change.
  • Peripheral route: attractiveness/emotion; less stable.

Cognitive dissonance: discomfort from conflicting attitudes/behaviors → change attitude or behavior to reduce dissonance.

CLEP Trap: Dissonance is most likely when behavior conflicts with beliefs and the person feels responsible.

3) Conformity, Compliance, Obedience

  • Conformity: adjust behavior to match group norms.
  • Compliance: change behavior due to a request.
  • Obedience: follow direct orders from authority.

Classic studies:

  • Asch: conformity in line judgments.
  • Milgram: obedience to authority (shock experiment).

CLEP Trap: Asch = peer pressure; Milgram = authority.

4) Group Dynamics

  • Social facilitation: better performance on simple/well-learned tasks in presence of others.
  • Social loafing: reduced effort in groups when individual contribution isn’t identifiable.
  • Deindividuation: loss of self-awareness/inhibitions in group anonymity.
  • Groupthink: pressure for harmony → poor decision-making.
  • Group polarization: group decisions become more extreme.

CLEP Trap: polarization = more extreme; groupthink = unanimity pressure + bad decisions.

5) Interpersonal Attraction

  • Similarity, proximity, mere exposure, reciprocity.

6) Aggression and Prosocial Behavior

  • Aggression influenced by biology, frustration, learning, and environment.
  • Bystander effect: more witnesses → less likely to help.
  • Diffusion of responsibility: responsibility spreads across group.

7) Stereotypes, Prejudice, Discrimination

  • Stereotype: belief about group.
  • Prejudice: attitude/feeling about group.
  • Discrimination: behavior toward group.

MODULE 13 – STATISTICS, TESTS, AND MEASUREMENT (3–4%)

College Board scope: descriptive statistics; inferential statistics; measurement of intelligence; reliability and validity; samples/populations/norms; types of tests.

1) Descriptive Statistics

Central tendency:

  • Mean: average.
  • Median: middle score (best for skew/outliers).
  • Mode: most frequent.

Formulas:

  • Mean: (\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n})

Variability:

  • Range: max − min.
  • Standard deviation (SD): average spread around mean (conceptual for CLEP).

Normal distribution: bell curve.

  • About 68% within 1 SD; ~95% within 2 SD (rule of thumb).

CLEP Trap: mean is not always best—outliers pull mean; median resists outliers.

2) Inferential Statistics (Conceptual)

  • Using a sample to infer about a population.
  • Statistical significance (p-value) indicates whether results likely due to chance.

CLEP level: distinguish “describe data” vs “infer/compare/test hypothesis.”

3) Reliability vs Validity

  • Reliability: consistency.
  • test–retest reliability, internal consistency.
  • Validity: accuracy (measures what it claims).
  • content validity, criterion validity, construct validity.

High-yield rule: a test can be reliable but not valid; valid tests must be reliable enough.

4) Z-scores (Occasional)

  • Z-score: (z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma})

Interpretation: how many SDs a score is above/below the mean.

5) Types of Tests / Norms

  • Norm-referenced: compares to population norms.
  • Criterion-referenced: compares to fixed standard.
  • Aptitude: predicts future performance.
  • Achievement: measures learned knowledge.
